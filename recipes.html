Twice-Fried Beef Tallow Fries

Classic double-cook for glassy crunch and creamy centers.

Story

I learned this method from an old Chicago diner cook who swore by beef tallow. When I moved to Denver, I found a local ranch that provides the perfect fat - grass-fed, rich, and with just enough minerality. The first fry at 325°F sets the structure, while the second at 375°F creates that signature glass-like crunch. It's the same technique used by the great fry masters of Belgium, but with a distinctly Colorado twist.

Ingredients

  • 4 large russet potatoes
  • Beef tallow (enough for deep frying)
  • Kosher salt

Method

  1. Cut fries, soak 30 min, rinse, and dry thoroughly.
  2. Blanch at 325°F until tender but pale (about 4–5 min). Drain and cool.
  3. Final fry at 375°F until deeply golden and crisp. Season immediately.

Garlic Aioli

Silky emulsion with pungent garlic bite.

Story

This recipe comes from my time working in a Catalan restaurant in Chicago where we made aioli by hand daily. The key is using Colorado garlic - it's got a sweeter, more complex flavor than California garlic. I'll never forget the chef teaching me to pound the garlic with salt in a mortar until it becomes a paste, watching the emulsion come together drop by drop. It's a technique that requires patience but rewards with the creamiest, most flavorful aioli you'll ever taste.

Ingredients

  • 2 large egg yolks
  • 4 garlic cloves
  • 1 tsp Dijon mustard
  • 1 cup neutral oil
  • 1 tbsp lemon juice
  • Salt to taste

Method

  1. Pound garlic and salt into smooth paste in mortar.
  2. Whisk in yolks and mustard until pale and thick.
  3. Slowly drizzle in oil while whisking constantly.
  4. Adjust seasoning with lemon juice and salt.

Spätzle

German egg noodles with perfect chew.

Story

As a chef when you are working through the ranks you come across mentors that shape your food journey and open up your culinary horizons. Peter Stiegler for me was one of those people and I was in charge of preparing the spaetzle every night at his restaurant Steiglers. Peter, who was the best leader I ever met, always reminded me every night to never be shy with the seasoning.

Ingredients

  • 2 cups (260g) all-purpose flour
  • 4 large eggs
  • ½ cup (120ml) whole milk (plus 1–2 Tbsp if needed)
  • 1 tsp kosher salt
  • Freshly ground black pepper
  • 2–3 Tbsp butter (for finishing)
  • Chives or parsley, finely chopped (optional)

Method

  1. Bring a large pot of well-salted water to a rolling boil.
  2. Whisk eggs and milk; add flour, salt, and a few grinds of pepper. Beat vigorously until a thick, elastic batter forms (it should fall slowly from a spoon). If too stiff, add a splash of milk.
  3. Working in batches, press batter through a spätzle maker or the large holes of a sturdy colander directly into the boiling water.
  4. Cook 1–2 minutes until the noodles float and look set. Scoop into an ice bath or a lightly oiled tray to stop cooking. Repeat with remaining batter.
  5. To serve, melt butter in a wide pan until foamy. Add spätzle and sauté 2–3 minutes until lightly golden at the edges. Season assertively (don't be shy!) and finish with herbs.

Chef Notes

  • Season your boiling water like pasta water—it matters.
  • Beat the batter hard: gluten development gives that bouncy chew.
  • Batch the boil, then finish all portions à la minute in butter for the best texture.
